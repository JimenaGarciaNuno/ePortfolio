01 — About Me

My Story

PCB Design Embedded Systems Georgia Tech SHPE Optica ASHRAE Bilingual FRC Mentor

Who I am: My name is Jimena Garcia-Nuno and I'm a first-year Electrical Engineering student at Georgia Institute of Technology. I'm a native Spanish speaker, fluent in English, and learning French. Growing up bilingual and bicultural taught me how to communicate across differences, a skill I carry into every engineering team I join.

What drew me to electrical engineering was a deep curiosity about the hardware behind technologies that shape our world — from microscopy systems used in cancer detection to the sensor networks that power smart infrastructure. Georgia Tech is exactly the right place to turn that curiosity into real-world impact.

Since arriving at GT, I've contributed to two active research labs. In the Optical Imaging and Spectroscopy Lab, I design and fabricate custom PCBs for biomedical imaging systems, develop probe prototypes in SolidWorks, and program microcontrollers using Arduino and MATLAB. At the Aerospace System Design Laboratory (ASDL), I've redesigned PCBs for a HUB and Spoke IoT sensor platform, improving hardware reliability for field deployment.

Outside of research, I serve as a Technical Mentor for FIRST Robotics Competition Team 6023, guiding high school students through system design, wiring, and hands-on engineering. I'm also an active member of the Society of Hispanic Professional Engineers, ASHRAE, and Optica.

What makes me stand out: I bring hands-on hardware fluency — soldering, PCB fabrication, continuity testing, and embedded programming — paired with the communication skills needed to thrive in diverse, interdisciplinary teams.

ArduinoPod
ECE 1100 Discovery Project

Arduino MP3 Player

Overview: A functional Arduino-based MP3 player that plays one of my favorite songs on startup. It features a display that shows the song name, along with buttons to control volume, skip tracks, and pause/play.

Process & Methods: Built using Arduino hardware and the Arduino IDE, with guidance from YouTube tutorials, GitHub repositories, and Claude for troubleshooting questions along the way.

Results: The MP3 player works fully and can even run on its own battery pack. I didn't get the chance to solder it onto a PCB to make it portable enough to carry as a keychain, but that's a future goal!

What I Learned: I got an introduction to serial communication and gained a much better appreciation for how sound can actually be produced from electronics — seeing it come together was pretty cool.
